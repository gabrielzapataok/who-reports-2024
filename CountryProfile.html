Overview of investment

The Seventy-fifth World Health Assembly adopted resolution WHA75.5 in May 2022, revising the approved base programmes segment of the Programme budget 2022-2023 to a total of US$ 6.726 billion. By the end of the first year, the total Programme budget has a good level of financing (US$ 8.3 billion), including projections, which exceeded the total approved. The good level of financing is explained by two event-driven budget segments: emergency operations and appeals, and polio eradication, the financing for which has needed to exceed the amounts established in the Programme budget in order to keep step with operational needs. , the financing for which has needed to exceed the amounts established in the Programme budget in order to keep step with oping for which has needed to exceed the amounts established in the Programme budget in order to keep step with operational needs.

Example 1-BUSOGA The baseline survey done for the WHO/KOICA RMNCAH health systems strengthening in February 2021 which included the targeted 5 districts of Bugiri, Buyende, Iganga, Kamuli and Mayuge, showed that overall, the skills, equipment and medicines required for implementation of MCH interventions in the five districts were lacking. All the five districts of Busoga region have documented relatively poor reproductive health indicators such as low contraceptive use by married women at 31.5%, high unmet need for family planning at 36.5%, low 4th antenatal visit attendance and delivery at 66% including low childhood immunization coverage at 44.9% amongst others. Therefore, WHO set out to improve the skills and knowledge of health workers, improve referral, infrastructure, and equipment. Currently a critical mass of health workers has been trained in various skills areas of Reproductive and Maternal health service delivery. This includes PAC-158, CEmONC- 359, BEmONC – 159, Management of Pregnancy, delivery, and post-natal care – 359, Family Planning-159 and Kangaroo Mother Care – 333 health workers. Seven ambulances are being supported for improved referral of mothers and newborns with obstetric and newborn complications. The project has also targeted the vulnerable adolescents in the Region. Data available for Uganda indicates that Busoga region ranks highest regarding teenage pregnancy rates in the country. WHO supported the training of 1939 teachers in SRHR information and oriented 7256 students in life skills and SRHR information and availability of services: covering a total of over 100 schools in 5 districts in the Busoga region. Relevant IEC materials covering various SRHR topics have been procured and distributed to all these schools, learners and teachers. PHOTO PROPOSED -Ambulance in Busoga with a woman being transported for higher care Cathy statement: “Our mothers and babies have been saved from trucks and boda bodas” -Voices of beneficiaries of WHO support in Busoga district where ambulances were supplied: But it isn’t just the availability of an ambulance that is making a difference. “Our health workers now know when to re-fer a patient to the next level. This has reduced delays at centres where patients would not be able to receive adequate care for their condition, The ambulance doesn’t simply pick patients from general hospitals to regional ones. “We pick patients from lower-level health centres and evacuate them to the facility that can handle them,” says Abel Kauzi, the 35-year-old emergency nurse working with the ambulance service team. “Most of our lower -level facilities don’t have neonatal intensive care units, so we bring a lot of babies to the hospital here,” he adds. LESSONS LEARNT • WCO local mobilisation of resources enabled achievement of planned activities • Being able to convince Ministry of health to adopt the WHO evidence-based norms and standards, tools and guidelines for use in their day-to-day programs, care and service delivery, has raised the profile and visibility of WHO in the country • Timely provision of technical guidance in emergency situations even in draft form is critical to maintain WHO’s role as a trusted and reliable source of evidence based guidance as was done in the development of the Continuity of Services during the both the COVID and Ebola responses
